Lab 2: Calculating Backgrounds¶

Part 1: Gamma Rays with Cosmic Ray Background (Problem 1)¶

Imagine we are looking for Gamma ray sources where our detector is a telescope. Our telescope is also sensitive to cosmic rays--consisting of protons and electrons--which produce a Poisson background distribution.

We shall assume that our background Poisson distribution of cosmic rays is 3 rays/day, and the average number of Gamma rays emitted by our source is 7 rays/day. Below we explore how our background distribution evolves as we collect more days of data; we then answer the question If we see 21 rays in 3 days, what is the probability that the background distribution for 3 days could have produced this many rays or more?

1.1 How probability distribution changes with number of days (Problem 1A)¶

Recall that our cosmic ray background is a Poisson distribution with an average rate of $\lambda = 3$ rays/day. (See Lab 1 for a detailed explanation of Poisson distributions).

$$P(k\space cosmic \space rays \space per \space day) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!} = \frac{3^k e^{-3}}{k!}$$

Suppose we take measurements over multiple days. What are the background distributions we expect to see? We expect to see a sum of the measurements we could see each day; we need to convolve the background with itself n-1 times if we take data over n days:

In [181]:
#set up cosmic ray background parameters

avgCosmic = 3 #assume background is Poisson with 3 rays/day
x = np.arange(0,30,1) #we make the tail long enough to avoid errors in convolutions
background1day = stats.poisson.pmf(x, avgCosmic)

figure()
plt.step(x,background1day)

plt.ylabel('probability mass function', size = 20)
plt.tick_params(labelsize = 20)
plt.xlim([0,20])

numDays = 5 #will plot 5, 15, 25 days of background
maxDays = 25
fig, ax = plt.subplots(1,1)
plt.title('Figure 2: Cosmic Background: '+ str(numDays)+' - '+str(maxDays) +' day convolutions',size = 20)
plt.xlabel('number of events in interval', size = 20)
plt.ylabel('probability mass function',size = 20)
plt.tick_params(labelsize = 20)

while numDays <= maxDays:
    multDays = background1day
    for i in range(numDays-1): #convolve background numDays-1 times
        multDays = np.convolve(multDays, background1day)
    xplot = np.arange(0, len(multDays),1) #in order to plot we need to know how long our convolved vector is
    ax.step(xplot, multDays, label = str(numDays) + ' day interval')
    leg = ax.legend()
    numDays = numDays + 10
plt.xlim([0,125])
plt.show()

We see that as we sum our background data over more days, our distribution shifts to the right, with a peak at $(3 \times \# days$). This is reasonable because a constant average rate (which is required for a distribution to be poisson) implies that the most likely number of events in multiple days should be the most likely number of events in one day multiplied by the number of days over which we collect data.

Additionally, the spread of the distribution increases; as we gather data over multiple intervals, there are more ways in which we can sum to get a number near the peak. (See homework 2 for a discussion on convolutions and summing dice rolls).

1.2 : Convolution of Poisson is still Poisson (Problem 1B)¶

Another interesting feature we observe in Figure 2 is that as the background is summed over multiple days, the distributions seems to remain Poisson.

In fact, we expect a convolution of Poisson distributions to remain Poisson because this distribution arises when we count how many times a discrete event occurs in a fixed interval, knowing that the event has a constant average rate. Thus, if we look at the data for 5 days, what we are doing is counting how many times cosmic rays hit a detector in 5 days. Since the average rate of cosmic rays per day (3 rays/day) is constant, the average rate of rays per 5 days is also constant (3 rays/day = 15 rays/5 days). Hence we expect our 5 day distribution to simply be a Poisson with mean $\lambda = 15$. Let's graph the two and check that they are the same:

1.3 Average Background over Multiple Days (Problem 1C)¶

In the previous section we looked at how the background distribution changes as we summed the background over multiple days. We want to take a look at how the average background distribution changes as we take multiple days of data. To do this, we simply have to sum up the background of n days, and then divide by the number of days we took measurements. Averageing the background over n days is really just a rescaling of the convolved distrubtions we saw in section 1.2:

    xplot = np.arange(0, len(multDays)/numDays,1/numDays) #AVERAGE MEANS RESCALING

We see that that the average background distribution shifts less to the right as we average over many days than if we simply summed over multiple days; In fact, the peak moves closer and closer to the average rate 3 rays/day. Additionally, the distriubtion becomes more symmetric and appears to be apporaching a smooth bell-shaped curve. This is inline with the Central Limit Theorem: as we sum the background over more and more days, the more our distribution will look like the Gaussian distribution. Note from our discussion in section 1.2 that this also implies that the Poisson will approach a Gaussian distribution as we let the average rate $\lambda \rightarrow \infty$ -- as we let our interval over which we calculate the the consant rate go to infinity.

1.4 Example Evaluation of Possible Gamma Ray Detection (Problem 1D)¶

Suppose we collect data for three days and our telescope ends up seeing 21 rays. We shall evaluate whether or not this is likely to be a signal from our Gamma source or something produced by the background.

Recall that our background for one day is a Poisson distribution with an average rate $\lambda = 3$ rays/day, and we know that our Gamma source produces, on average, 7 rays/day.

Now, our telescope has measured 21 events in these three days. We want to know how likely it is that this signal was produced by our background, and what the significance of our measurement is:

What is the probability that my background distribution for three days produced a signal that is greater than or equal to 21 events in three days? Furthermore, if my background were normally distributed, how far away from the mean, in units of standard deviation, would my signal have to be in order to produce this same probability?

We carry out the calculation below. (see Lab 1 for a discussion on the cdf and ppf functions).

In [78]:
#calculate probablity that cosmic background produced 21 events in 3 days:

#below we use the fact that collecting 3 days of data is a Poisson distribution
#with 3 times the mean of 1 day of data; avgCosmic is the average rate for 1 day of cosmic rays
cosmicProb = 1 - stats.poisson.cdf(21,3*avgCosmic)

#below we calculate the significance
significance = stats.norm.ppf(1-cosmicProb)

print('Probability Cosmic rays produced 21 events or more in 3 days = ', cosmicProb)
print('Significance = ', significance, '\u03C3')

Probability Cosmic rays produced 21 events or more in 3 days =  0.00017495107488796346
Significance =  3.5752434478403172 σ

Hence we see that the probability that our background produced a signal of 21 events or more in 3 days is very low: 0.0175%. If our background were normally distributed, we would have to be at least 3.57 standard deviations to the right of the mean in order to produce such a low probability. Unfortunately, the physics community generally requires a significance of 5$\sigma$ in order to claim a discovery, so we cannot claim such a victory in this case.

Part 2: Exponential Distribution and the Central Limit Theorem¶

In this section we will explore how the Exponential Distribution changes shape as we average the distribution over many intervals.

The exponential distribution is such that if X is a random variable that is Poisson distributed with rate $\lambda$, then the waiting time between events X is exponentially distributed with rate parameter $\lambda$ : P(t) = $\lambda e^{-\lambda t}$

Part 3: Introducing Trial Factors (Problem 3)¶

In this problem we assume we are working with the UV/infra-red satellite SWIFT in order to corroborate neutron star merger signals seen by LIGO. Our detector consists of an optical telescope with an array of CCD's, each connected to a pixel, where we shall consider the signal from each individual CCD to be a trial.

We assume that each pixel has a Gaussian background centered at 0, with standard deviation 2.4 (see Figure 13 below)

3.1: One Trial (Problem 3 Version 1)¶

In this scenario we imagine that our SWIFT detector picks up a signal at the same time as LIGO. Because of SWIFT's accuracy, we know exactly which pixel to check in the image taken to see a possible signal. We assume we see a signal of 7.3. Our question is:

What is the probability that the Gaussian background of the pixel produced a signal of 7.3 or higher?

We perform the calculations to answer the question below:

#integrate background distribution from 7.3 to infinity to get probability that background produced signal
backsignal = 1 - stats.norm.cdf(7.3,loc = 0, scale = 2.4)
sig = stats.norm.ppf(1 - backsignal)

print('probability background produced signal of 7.3 or higher = ', backsignal)
print('significance = ', sig, '\u03C3')

probability background produced signal of 5.3 or higher =  0.0011763613784413485
significance =  3.041666666666669 σ

Unfortunately we cannot claim a discovery since we require a significance of 5 $\sigma$.

3.2: Ten Thousand Trials (Problem 3 Version 2)¶

We now assume that LIGO recieves a signal while SWIFT is indisposed. Because of this, we know which region of the sky to look in but we do not know which pixel will contain the signal. Hence, we need to search for a signal among 10,000 pixels. This means that we have 10,000 trials.

How do we determine our background if we have 10,000 trials? The answer is pretty straightforward: we must multiply the original distribution by the number of trials. This follows from the fact that we are really only checking one pixel at a time so we must ask questions of the type what is the probability I got a signal from pixel 1 OR the probability I got a signal from pixel 2 OR...OR I got a got a signal from pixel 10,000?

As opposed to section 1, we are not looking at the sum of data from our detectors, we are simply checking 10,000 different detectors one at time. We refer to the number of trials as the trial factor.

Below we display the background of one pixel, and the background of 10,000 pixels:

Notice that the distribution for 10,000 pixels is not really a probability density function, but rather a measure of roughly how many pixels are expected to give off a particular signal.

So instead of asking the question what is the probability that my background produced by 10,000 pixels could have produced a signal at 7.3 or more?, we ask:

what is the expected number of pixels, out of 10,000, that might produce a false signal of 7.3 or greater?

To answer this question we must integrate the 10,000 pixel background from 7.3 to infinity, which is the same as integrating a one-pixel probability distribution and multiplying by 10,000:

Part 4: How Trials Affect Signal Strength Required (Problem 4)¶

4.1 Signal Required for 5$\sigma$ Detection From One Trial (Problem 4A)¶

Assume we are in the same scenario as section 3.1: we know exactly which pixel to look at, so we just need to consider the background of one pixel. Recall that this background is a Gaussian centered at 0 with a standard deviation of 2.4. We are interested in answering the question:

How strong does a signal--from the pixel we know to check--have to be to have a significance of 5$\sigma$?

Since our background is Gaussian centered about 0, the answer is easily calculated: our signal must be 5 $\times$ the standard deviation (2.4) to the right of the mean. Hence the signal must be greater than or equal to $0+ 5\times 2.4 = 12$.

We perform the calculation for a more general distribution below:

In [174]:

#we use the standard normal since cdf depends on the distance away from the mean of any normal distribution
#(see lab 1 for further explanation):
sig5 = 1 - stats.norm.cdf(5,loc = 0, scale = 1)


#now use the percent point function to find the associated threshold on your particular distribution
#our distribution is gaussian in this case but with a standard deviation of 2.4
threshold = stats.norm.ppf(1 - sig5, loc = 0, scale = 2.4)

print('5\u03C3 probability = ', sig5)
print('signal must be >= ', threshold)

5σ probability =  2.866515719235352e-07
signal must be >=  11.999999999928422

We see that our more general algorithm corroborates the answer we calculated above using simple arithmetic.

4.2 Signal Required for 5$\sigma$ Detection from 10,000 Trials (Problem 4B)¶

Let us now assume we are in the same scenario as 3.2: we don't know which pixel to look at, so we need to consider the background of 10,000 pixels. Since this amounts to having 10,000 different experiments (the signal can only come from one pixel), our new background is 10,000 times as 'bright'. So our new background is 10,000*(1-pixel background).

We are now interested in answering the question:

How strong does a signal -- originating from one pixel among 10,000-- have to be to have a significance of 5$\sigma$?

In order to call the stats.norm.ppf() function, which does not take in a parameter for a trials factor, we need to calculate some parameters.

We know that $$10,000 - cdf_{10,000 \space trials}(x) = Prob(signal \geq x),$$
since the total integral from $-\infty$ to $+\infty$ must be 10,000 since we multiplied a Gaussian (which is normalized) by 10,000.

Now, since cdf() is just an integral from $-\infty$ to x, and our new distribution is simply 10,000*(old distribution), then it must be the case that

$$cdf_{10,000 \space trials}(x) = 10,000 \space cdf_{1 \space trial}(x),$$

where $cdf_{1 \space trial}(x)$ is simply stats.norm.cdf(x) in this particular case.

Hence:

$$stats.norm.cdf(x) = \frac{10,000 - Prob(signal \geq x)}{10,000}.$$

Thus in order to call $stats.norm.ppf(q)$ which is the inverse of $stats.norm.cdf_{1\space trial}(x),$ we must make sure that our argument is of the form $1 - \frac{Prob(signal \geq x)}{10,000}$ rather than simply $1-x$ (which is what we would input if we were looking at just 1 trial).

We perform the calculation below:

However, it's important to note that even though our trials increased by a factor of 10,000, our signal strength only increased by a factor of 1.31. Let's try another example; notice once again that the threshold increases by a small amount in comparison to the increase in number of trials:

Here we see that although the trials increased by a factor of 10,000,000, the threshold for 5$\sigma$ only increased by a factor of 1.5. In general, the threshold sensitivity for Gaussian backgrounds is small around $5\sigma$ significance thresholds because the pdf() function is falling rapidly enough so that the cdf() function is relatively constant in the region we consider: $$\frac{d}{dx} \int_{-\infty}^{x} e^{-x^2}\space dx = e^{-x^2}\rightarrow 0 \space monotonically \space as\space x \rightarrow \infty,$$ and $$e^-25 = 1.39\space 10^-11,$$ so we may consider $$\frac{d}{dx}cdf(x) \approx 0 \space \forall x \geq 5\sigma.$$ Hence cdf(x) is almost constant in this region. This means that the area under the curve grows very slowly past 5$\sigma$. If we multiply the curve by $N << Prob(5\sigma)$, we still have a rapidly falling curve so that even though the height of the pdf() is N times higher at every point, it does not take $N 5\sigma$ to reach the point where we can consider the cdf() to be relatively constant.
