datasize = 10000 #10,000 measurements
numsignals = 500 #inject signal 500 times
noise = stats.poisson.rvs(100,loc = 0, size = datasize)
signals = np.zeros(datasize)

#pick random indices between 0 and datasize to inject signal
indices = np.random.randint(10000,size = numsignals)

#set signals array to be 190 (8 sigma) at these indices
for i in indices:
    signals[i] = 190
    

data = noise + signals
    
figure()
plt.hist(data,bins = 100)
plt.title('Figure 1: 10,000 Measurements with 500 signals centered at 190 events/interval \n Poisson Background')
plt.xlabel('Numer of Events in One Interval')
plt.ylabel('Occurences')
plt.show()

Notice that the observed signal is not biased because the noise is symmetric and average signal strength (190) is far from the background average (100).

Also notice that although we injected the signal 500 times, the height of the signal 'bump' is only about 200 counts; because we have a finite width to our signal distribution, we get fewer counts at the true signal. In order to get a brighter signal, we would need to insert more counts.

Problem 3¶

We now wish to 'zoom in' to the histogram centered about the injected signals that we saw in the above two figures. We would like to inject true signals from 0$\sigma$ to somewhere around 30$\sigma$ and see how the histograms of the observed signals vary.

In order to avoid implicit biasing of signals, we inject linearly spaced signals: 0,1,2,...,999 into our Poisson background centered at 100.

We have 1000 measurements; for each signal strength we a random number generated from a Poisson background centered at 100. That is, for each signal strength we add a random poisson number 1000 times to get a distrubtion about the signal strength.

We then make a 2D histogram, where the x and y axes are the observed signals and the injected signals:

The above histogram tells us that the most likely injected signal, given an observed signal of 600, is somewhere around 500 events/interval. It also tells us that there is a nonzero chance of having a true signal of 480 or 520 and getting an observed signal of 600.

However, it is more likely that our true signal is close to the 500 events/interval. Additionally there is no bias: there is no reason why our true signal is more likely to be higher than 500 than it is to be lower than 500.

We would now like to find the exact value of the expected true value, and the error bars associated with the experimental value. Our error bars are associated with $1\sigma$ probability; the right error bar tells you that your true signal will be above the experimental mean and within the given range about 33% of the time, while the left error bar tells you that your true signal will be below the experimental mean and within the given range about 33% of the time.

for j in bins1:

    if difference < difference1:

        start = j

if I observe this candidate signal at 107 counts, what is the upper bound on my true signal for 95% of the time?
