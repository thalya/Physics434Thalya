Part 1¶

Suppose we have two six-sided dice. What is more probable: rolling two ones or rolling two numbers that sum to 7?

Since we have 6 dice there are exactly $6 \times 6 = 36$ different combinations $(X,Y)$ where $X \in \{1,2,...,6\}$ is the number rolled by the first die, and $Y \in \{1,2,...,6\}$ is the number rolled by the second die.

There is exactly one way to roll two ones: $(X,Y) = (1,1)$, therefore the probability of rolling two ones is 1/36.

There are 6 different ways of rolling two numbers that add up to 7:
$(X,Y) \in \{(1,6),\space(2,5),\space(3,4),\space(4,3),\space(5,2),\space(6,1)\}$. Therefore the probability of rolling two numbers that sum to 7 is 6/36 = 1/6.

Hence the probability of rolling two ones is smaller than the probability of rolling a sum of 7.

Part 2¶

How do we use the convolution operator to find the probabilities for the possible sums of two dice?

2.1 Analytic Method¶

We saw in Part 1 that the probability of rolling two numbers that add up to 7 was 1/36. We did this by counting all the possible combinations $(X,Y)$ and then finding all the combinations that satisfy our condition $X+Y = 7$.

We could have rephrased the question as what is the probability of measuring a 1 first and a 6 second; or a 2 first and 5 second; or a 3 first and a 4 second;...;or a 6 first and a 1 second?. To answer this question we would have had to compute 6 different probabilities by taking the product of the two probabilites involved, and then summed them up: $$P(sum \space of\space 7) = \frac{1}{6} \frac{1}{6} +\frac{1}{6} \frac{1}{6}+...+\frac{1}{6}\frac{1}{6} \space (6 times) = 6 \times \frac{1}{36},$$ where we have used the fact that $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$.

The convolution function is a way of first imposing the condition $X+Y = 7$ and then summing over $P(X)\times P(Y)$, much like we did in the rephrasing of the question above:

$$P(X+Y=7) = (P_X* P_Y)(7) = \sum_{z=1}^{z=6}P_X(z)\space P_Y(7-z).$$

Since $P_X(z)\space P_Y(7-z)$ means Probability of rolling a (z) first and a (7-z) second which is equivalent to the condition that the two numbers must add up to 7. Note that we let $z$ range from 1 to 6 since those are the only possibilities on both dice; i.e. $P_X(z) = P_Y(z) = 0$ if $z \notin \{1,2,...,6\}$.

Now, if we generalize our question to what is the probability of rolling a sum of N with two dice?, we simply have to perform the convolution $(P_X * P_Y)(N) = \sum_{z=1}^{z=6}P_X(z)\space P_Y(N-z)$.

We can simplify this further since $P_X(z) = \frac{1}{6} \forall z \in \{1,2,..,6\}$: $$(P_X * P_Y)(N) = \frac{1}{6} \sum_{z=1}^{z=6}P_Y(N-z).$$

Example

what is the probability of rolling two numbers that sum to 1? what is the probability of rolling two numbers that sum to 9?

The probability of rolling a sum of 1 is: $$(P_X * P_Y)(1) = \frac{1}{6} \sum_{z=1}^{z=6}P_Y(1-z) = \frac{1}{6}(P_Y(0)+P_Y(-1)+P_Y(-2)+...+P_Y(-5)) = 0.$$

The Probability of rolling a sum of 9 is: $$(P_X * P_Y)(9) = \frac{1}{6} \sum_{z=1}^{z=6}P_Y(1-z) = \frac{1}{6}(P_Y(8)+P_Y(7)+P_Y(6)+...+P_Y(3)) = 0 = \frac{1}{6}(0+0+P_Y(6)+ P_Y(5)+P_Y(4)+P_Y(3)) = \frac{1}{6} \frac{4}{6} = \frac{4}{36}.$$

It is up to the user to keep track of the axes they choose and which probabilities correspond to which sum. Let us illustrate what we mean:

Part 3¶

What is the mean and variance of the distribution found in section 2?

In order to calculate the average sum that would appear if two dice wer rolled, we need to weigh each possible outcome by its probability:

$$mean = \frac{\sum_{s = -\infty}^{s=\infty}P(s) s}{\sum_{-\infty}^{\infty}P(s)} = $$


$$\sum_{s = -\infty}^{s=\infty}P(s) s,$$

where P(s) is the probability mass distribution given by the convolution of the two individual die probabilities (see Part 3), and the total probability is equal to 1. For two dice, s has a non-zero probability for values between s=2 and s=12. Hence: $$mean = \sum_{s=2}^{s=12}P(s) s.$$

The code below computes this for us:

We see that that the average sum rolled by two dice is 7, which make sense since the distribtion pictured in Figure 1 is symmetric about 7 so expect the average to be there.

In order to calculate the variance we must weigh the square of each measurement's distance from the mean by its respective probability:

$$variance = \frac{\sum_{s = -\infty}^{s=\infty}P(s) (s-mean)^2}{\sum_{-\infty}^{\infty}P(s)} = $$


$$\sum_{s = -\infty}^{s=\infty}P(s) (s-mean)^2,$$

where P(s) is the probability mass distribution given by the convolution of the two individual die probabilities (see Part 3), and the total probability is equal to 1. For two dice, s has a non-zero probability for values between s=2 and s=12. Hence: $$variance = \sum_{s=2}^{s=12}P(s) (s-mean)^2.$$

The code below computes this for us:

Part 4¶

How do we use the convolution operator to find the probabilities for the possible sums of ten dice dice?

4.1 Analytic Method¶

If we ask what is the probability of getting a sum of 17 when rolling 10 dice? we can rephrase the question -- as we did in section 2.1 -- to read what is the probability of rolling 9 numbers that sum to 16 and then rolling a 1; or rolling 9 numbers that sum to 15 and then rolling a 2;...;or rolling 9 numbers that sum to 11 and then rolling a 6?

Comparing this question to the one we saw in section 2.1, we see that the answer to this question is given by by the convolution between the distribution for 9 dice and the distribution for one die:

$$P(10\space dice\space summing\space to \space 17) = (P_{9\space dice \space sum} * P_{1 \space die})(17) = \sum_{z=0}^{z=\infty}P_{9\space dice\space sum}(z)P_{1 \space die}(17-z)$$

But the question what is the probability of rolling 9 numbers that sum to 16? is the same as asking what is the probability of rolling 8 numbers that sum to 15 and then a 1; or rolling 8 numbers that sum to 14 and then a 2;...; or rolling 8 numbers that sum to 10 and then a 6?

So for each z that contributes to teh sum above, we can similarly break down $P_{9\space die \space sum}$ into a convolution of an 8 dice sum distribution and a 1 dice distribution. Hence we can write: $$P(10\space dice\space summing\space to \space 17) = (P_{1 \space die} * P_{1 \space die}* ...*P_{1 \space die})(17)$$ where we have performed nine convolutions between 10 identical dice.

4.2 Numeric Method¶

Though the concept is easy to understand, performing 9 convolutions by hand would be time consuming. Luckily we can call on our numpy.convolve() function to speed things up. Figure 3 and Figure 4 are very suggestive of a Gaussian distribtuions: the log scale certainly looks like a negative quadratic which is indicative of a Gaussian distribution. However, a Gaussian distribution is continuous and this is discrete, so it cannot be exactly equal to the Gaussian.

However, according to the central limit theorem, if we consider samples of independent and identically distributed random variables (e.g. N identical dice), then as the sample size gets larger, the distribution of sample averages will approximate the normal distribution. This means as we toss more dice, we can better approximate the distribution of averages by a normal distribution.
